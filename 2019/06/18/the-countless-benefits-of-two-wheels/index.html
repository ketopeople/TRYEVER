The Countless Benefits of Two Wheels


Since the early 1800s, humans have been tooling around on two wheels. While the bicycle has changed dramatically in looks, capabilities and price (with the most expensive going for as much as $500,000!), its basic mechanism remains the same: human-powered transportation.

What’s amazing is how we learn to ride a bicycle in the first place. Think about it – balancing on two skinny wheels at speeds up to 25 miles per hour on a flat surface and much faster downhill. And many of us can do it with no hands!

Scientists still don’t know exactly how our brains manage to figure out how to ride a bike, not to mention never forgetting once we’ve learned. One amazing study is of a man with Parkinson’s who was unable to walk even a few steps without falling, but once he got on a bicycle, he could ride with the greatest of ease. In fact, bicycle riding is used as therapy for people with Parkinson’s and other related neurological disorders.

But even people without disabilities can benefit immensely from riding a bike. Seniors in particular reap some very important rewards in doing so regularly.

Health Benefits of Riding a Bicycle

  • Low-impact exercise. Bicycling has been called the ultimate low-impact exercise because there is little pressure on the knees and ankles in the smooth, circular motion of pedaling.
  • Increases strength and stamina. You don’t have to do the Tour de France to build significant muscle, strength and endurance with regular bike riding. For those who have trouble pedaling long distances or uphill, there are electric bikes that can give a power boost when needed.
  • Prevents arthrosis, the degeneration of cartilage in the joint spaces. The circular motion of bicycling allows energy and nutrients to be distributed evenly throughout the body’s structures, including the cartilage that cushions and protects bone.
  • Good for the heart and reduces risk of disease. Regular bicycling can increase one’s cardiovascular function by 3-7 percent. It also reduces blood fat levels, which is the principal cause of heart disease. Other disorders, such as diabetes, kidney malfunction, skin and ocular diseases, skeletal problems, a compromised immune system, and some forms of cancer are mitigated by regular bike riding.
  • Weight control. An hour of bicycling riding burns an average of 300 calories, depending on one’s weight. Burning fat and building muscle are the main elements of weight control or loss, and bicycling does both wonderfully well. Biking also boosts metabolism, so riders continue to burn fat even after they’ve stopped pedaling.
  • Improves mental health. What’s better than getting outside with the wind at your back, especially if you’re riding with friends? Regular biking boosts levels of “feel good” hormones, such as serotonin, dopamine and phenylethylamine, thus increasing holistic well-being and warding off anxiety and depression.

Environmental Benefits
In countries like Denmark, where bicycling is a common form of transportation and infrastructure accommodates and encourages it, the environmental benefits of using a bike instead of a car are well known. Bicycles utilize minimal fossil fuels and create no pollution. They also ease the flow of traffic and minimize the automobile congestion that causes greenhouse gas emissions.

Economic Benefits
80 percent of the world’s population can afford a bicycle, whereas only 10 percent can afford a car. Riding a bicycle to get from here to there requires no money for gasoline either and most bicycle parking is free. Plenty of cities offer bike rentals at very affordable rates for people to make their way around town, to work or run errands.

Biking in Naperville and at Monarch Landing
A quick check of the Internet shows several bicycle clubs in Naperville. This makes good sense, given the city’s location near several branches of the Illinois Prairie Path, one of which is on the Naperville Riverwalk.

With 80 scenic acres, designated bike paths and the Prairie Path so nearby, Monarch Landing is truly the ideal place to ride a bicycle. Our community has an active biking club with 12-15 residents who ride together, weather permitting, twice per week and log 5-10 miles. One 85-years-young resident rides as many as 1,000 miles in a season!

Stay Safe!
Helmets reduce the risk of head and brain injury for all bicyclists by 88 percent, so never ride without one, even if you’re literally just going around the corner. In addition, don’t ride with headphones or music playing; be sure to have a horn or a bell; use proper signals; call out to people you’re going to pass; have identification and a phone with you in a secure bike pack; stick to designated bike paths or lanes and away from auto traffic; find the bike that’s best for you; and never ride in the dark without proper lighting and reflective clothing. It’s best not to ride in the dark at all, and it’s also good practice to ride with a buddy – or 12!
